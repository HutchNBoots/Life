Why this works
A quick read, not a literature review.
Why three good things?

In 2005, Martin Seligman and colleagues had one group write down three things that went well each day, for a week. A month later, that group was measurably happier — and the effect held at three and six months.

Worth knowing: not every replication has been clean. A 2018 meta-analysis found gratitude exercises average weaker effects than the original study suggested. Honest summary — one of the better-replicated small interventions in positive psychology, most reliable when done consistently.

Seligman, Steen, Park & Peterson, 2005 · American Psychologist
Why naming what's unresolved helps

In the 1920s, Bluma Zeigarnik noticed waiters remembered unpaid orders in far more detail than paid ones — unfinished tasks stay mentally "open." Later research found that simply writing a concrete plan for something unfinished reduces the intrusive thoughts it causes, even before it's done.

That's the logic of the "to sort" slot — naming the loose end does real work before it's resolved. "Sorted" closes the loop on something that was pulling at your attention.

Zeigarnik, 1920s · Masicampo & Baumeister, 2011
Why noticing good things does more than feel nice

Barbara Fredrickson's broaden-and-build theory proposes positive emotions briefly widen what you notice, compared to negative emotions narrowing focus onto a threat. Over time those small moments compound into resilience and better coping.

Fredrickson, 2001 · American Psychologist
Why streaks and small rewards

A 2010 study tracked 96 people forming a new daily habit — it took a median of 66 days to become automatic, ranging from 18 to 254. The biggest jumps happened early, then tapered off, which is why rewards here are frequent at first and space out later. Missing one day didn't meaningfully derail habit formation — which is part of why a broken streak resets quietly rather than punishing you.

Lally et al., 2010 · European Journal of Social Psychology
Most of this research measures mood over weeks to months, not a permanent shift — and none of it replaces therapy or treatment for depression or anxiety. It's a small, well-studied habit, not a clinical fix.
